# India-Border-Adjustment-Mechanism-IBAM-
A simulator for India's carbon sovereignty.
On 1 January 2026, the EU's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) came fully into force. Indian exporters of steel, cement, aluminium and fertiliser now face a carbon levy at Europe's border — money that, by default, leaves India entirely.

The problem
European producers enjoy generous free allowances and subsidised public finance. Indian producers do not. Without action, CBAM transfers India's decarbonisation burden — and its revenues — to European hands.

The IBAM proposal
Article 9 of the CBAM Regulation explicitly recognises a "carbon price paid in the country of origin". An India Border Adjustment Mechanism (IBAM), layered on the Carbon Credit Trading Scheme (CCTS), would levy an equivalent domestic charge — credited fully against CBAM. Net cost to the exporter stays capped; the revenue stays in India.

The constructive offer
Retained revenue should be ring-fenced in a transparent green fund: blast-furnace modernisation, green hydrogen, scrap-based steelmaking, low-carbon electricity, and worker transition — all independently audited and publicly disclosed.

What this tool does
A live, interactive policy dashboard that lets exporters, analysts and negotiators see — in rupees and tonnes — exactly how much carbon money stays in India under different scenarios.
